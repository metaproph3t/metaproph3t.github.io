Do we need money anymore?

People use money for a few reasons. For one, it allows them to store purchasing power. A baker can sell bread today and buy meat tomorrow. Money also gives people a unit of account in which to price goods and investments.

Cryptocurrency enthusiasts tend to be split on how money should look in the future. Some advocate for a synthetic commodity money, like BTC, ETH, SOL, or even DOGE. Proponents generally decry the injustices of central banks, including infringing on their citizens' privacy, increasing economic inequality, and printing too much money. On the other hand, others say that assets like BTC are far too volatile to be used by normal people, and that people should instead use stablecoins that are either pegged to individual fiat currencies, a basket of currencies, or a basket of assets. People who use these tokens will be able to get the benefits of blockchains (e.g., programmable contracts, instant global payments, etc.) without incurring market risk, or so they say.

What if they're all wrong? What if currency doesn't have a future? And no, I'm not being provocative for the sake of being provocative. Please allow me a brief moment to explain.

The general idea

As stated in the Terra Whitepaper, "Currencies inherently have strong network effects: a customer is unlikely to switch over to a new currency unless a critical mass of merchants are ready to accept it, but at the same time, merchants have no reason to invest resources and educate staff to accept a new currency unless there is significant customer demand for it." This is the premise upon which all currencies are based. When I walk into a store, I need to be carrying the currency that a merchant will accept. If I try to pay with Euros in an American supermarket, I'm going to have a tough time.

Or am I? Most debit and credit cards allow you to make free or very cheap (~1% fees) international purchases. So someone walking into an American supermarket with only Euros (in their bank account) would be okay: Visa or Mastercard will convert currencies in the back-end so that I can pay in Euros and the merchant can get paid in US Dollars. This generalizes to assets stored on blockchains as well. Coinbase Card, for example, allows you to spend using crypto. PayPal allows you to pay with crypto in checkout. From a technical implementation perspective, it should be trivial for these firms to allow users to pay using other blockchain-based assets.

Now look at an asset class that has existed since the 16th centry and is starting to move onto blockchains: commodity futures. Commodity futures are simply a way to buy a good before that good has been produced. For example, if I am a chicken farmer and you are McDonalds, I can pre-sell you a quantity of chickens before I have raised them.

While neither of these above ideas - paying with crypto and commodities futures - is particularly exciting by itself, paring them together might eliminate the need for money.
