DeFi: The 10,000 Foot View

In November 1910, a group of six powerful men met at a country club in Georgia. Their conference was most secretly-guarded: the men didn't publicly admit that it had happened until 20 years later. We might even call it a conspiracy. As far as conspiracies go, this one turned out to be fairly important: it led to the creation of the Federal Reserve System, which in turn became the world's de facto banking system.

We are in the midst of another conspiracy. This one is similar in intent - reshape the way that the financial system works - but different in character. Instead of 6 powerful American men, it's thousands of people from all over the world. Many of these people would be considered outsiders in normal society, with their strange mannerisms and their spouting of technobabble that seems to be a language into iself. Instead of meetings at country clubs, they coordinate over hackmds, Telegram group chats, and conference presentations. Many of them opt to be pseudonymous, including myself. And so far, their plan appears to be working: $45B has already migrated to the system they've created.

To be sure, $45B is a small amount when compared to the world's wealth, which stands at about $400T. But it's a start. And it's a hell of a lot more than $200M, which is what it was 4 years ago in 2019. In fact, DeFi usage is growing about as fast as internet bandwidth usage grew in the 1990s. An opportunity to be the next global financial system

We live in an increasingly globalized world. And yet our financial systems have yet to catch up. It is still hard, slow, and expensive to do the following things:

    • send assets to people in other jurisdictions
        exchange currencies
          directly purchase securities that have issuers outside of your jurisdiction
            invest in funds that are operated outside of your jurisdiction

            It is not hard to understand why. Each country has its own financial system, which can be understood as the combination of two ledgers: the money ledger, maintained by the central bank, and the financial instrument ledger, managed by a tighly-regulated securities depository like DTCC. Things are a little bit more complicated than this - for example, the US actually has 2 main financial instrument ledgers, since the Fed clears government securities - but this is the 10,000 foot view, right? Anyways, as any distributed systems engineer knows, these types of ledgers can only have one source of truth. What this means in practice is that money or financial instruments cannot flow across borders. US dollars cannot be stored on the Bank of England's ledger, nor can Indian Rupees be stored on the Fed's ledger. If you want to interact with the currency or financial instruments of a given jurisdiction, you must create an account with that jurisdiction's ledger(s) or with someone else who has access to the ledger (e.g., a bank, a broker, a payment processor, et cetera).

            Now, given that there are 196 countries it would obviously be crazy for people to create an account with every ledger. So historically, we've managed this by using a common hub: the US financial system. The vast majority of international trade, other than between EU member states, happens in US dollars. And the vast majority of the world's equities are purchasable by American brokers, either because the company has listed on an American exchange or via American depository receipts (ADRs). If you're a Saudi oil tycoon who wants to buy Nintendo stock, you trade will probably never touch Tokyo; instead, you will use US dollars to buy Nintendo ADRs through an NYC-based broker.
