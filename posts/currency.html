Do we still need currencies?

Context

Over milennia, many gallons of proverbial ink have been spilled over the question of money. Some of history's brightest minds have engaged in heated debates over topics such as who should control the money supply, whether currencies should be inflationary or deflationary, and what, if anything, currencies should be backed by.

These haven't been purely the musings of theoreticians, either. They have translated into policy actions such as the adoption and subsequent abandonment of the gold standard, governments' responses to the Great Depression and subsequent recessions, and governments' management of interest rates. Although the study of currencies is an abstract discipline, it has very real effects, impressing itself upon the lives of billions.

The cryptocurrency movement is a recent attempt to improve the way that currencies function. Some members of this movement advocate for a synthetic commodity money, like BTC, ETH, SOL, or even DOGE. These proponents generally decry the injustices of central banks, including infringing on their citizens' privacy, increasing economic inequality, and printing too much money. This is the thrust behind memes like 21 million and ultra sound money.

Others say that assets like BTC are far too volatile to be used for daily transactions, and that that people should instead use stablecoins that are either pegged to individual fiat currencies, a basket of currencies, or a basket of assets. They argue that stablecoins give users the benefits of blockchain (e.g., instant global payments, DeFi, access to NFTs, etc.) without the risk of their savings losing excessive value.

The price of bitcoins can fluctuate substantially, even over just one week

Others are opposed to deflationary assets like BTC for monetary reasons. Projects like Freicoin aim to encourage spending, thus bolstering the economy of any economy that uses freicoins as a medium of exchange, by making holders pay a 'demurrage fee' for holding freicoins.

Recently, I have started to wonder if they are all wrong. What if the problem that currencies solve no longer exists in a blockchain-based financial system (which I believe will gain traction[link])? What would a world without currencies look like?

The currency premise

Currencies exist as a common ground upon which to conduct commercial and financial transactions. If I'm a butcher and I would like to buy bread from the baker, I can't just walk in with a slab of meat and expect to walk out with a loaf of bread. What if, for example, the baker is a vegetarian?

Of course, it would be great if either of us had a magic machine which instantly converted what I have (meat) for whatever the baker wants (say, a cookbook for making samosas), but as we don't have that machine, we need to agree on some common thing to trade with: a money.

Thus far, this premise has been accepted by the cryptocurrency community. For example, the Terra Whitepaper states: "currencies inherently have strong network effects: a customer is unlikely to switch over to a new currency unless a critical mass of merchants are ready to accept it, but at the same time, merchants have no reason to invest resources and educate staff to accept a new currency unless there is significant customer demand for it."

DeFi can break this premise

People use money for a few reasons. For one, it allows them to store purchasing power. A baker can sell bread today and buy meat tomorrow. Money also gives people a unit of account in which to price goods and investments.

What if they're all wrong? What if currency doesn't have a future? And no, I'm not being provocative for the sake of being provocative. Please allow me a brief moment to explain.

The general idea

Or am I? Most debit and credit cards allow you to make free or very cheap (~1% fees) international purchases. So someone walking into an American supermarket with only Euros (in their bank account) would be okay: Visa or Mastercard will convert currencies in the back-end so that I can pay in Euros and the merchant can get paid in US Dollars. This generalizes to assets stored on blockchains as well. Coinbase Card, for example, allows you to spend using crypto. PayPal allows you to pay with crypto in checkout. From a technical implementation perspective, it should be trivial for these firms to allow users to pay using other blockchain-based assets.

Now look at an asset class that has existed since the 16th centry and is starting to move onto blockchains: commodity futures. Commodity futures are simply a way to buy a good before that good has been produced. For example, if I am a chicken farmer and you are McDonalds, I can pre-sell you a quantity of chickens before I have raised them.

While neither of these above ideas - paying with crypto and commodities futures - is particularly exciting by itself, paring them together might eliminate the need for money.
