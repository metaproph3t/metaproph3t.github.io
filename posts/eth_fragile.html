Why Ethereum's market position is more fragile than you think

The year is 2026. All applications and users have finally finished migrating from Ethereum Mainnet to a L2 rollup such as Optimism, Arbitrum, zkSync, or Scroll. Meanwhile, the price of ETH is plummeting. People are confused. This is the future they wanted; why isn't it leading to the outcome they wanted? 'Wat mean?' asks @RyanSAdams on XXX (Elon got a bit carried away with naming). Well, let me tell you wat mean.

Life as pictured through ETH maxi goggles

"All I see is ETH, ETH everywhere"

As detailed in Vitalik's Incomplete Guide to Rollups, rollups consume L1 blockspace in three ways:

  1. storing state roots
  2. publishing transaction calldata
  3. (in ZK rollups) on-chain verification of the ZK-SNARK

ETH maxis like this because it means that Ethereum users can pay cheaper prices without reducing the total amount of ETH burnt. It's like Ethereum is an airplane, and the L2 is a magical seating technology that allows more seats to fit on the plane. That way, the plane can charge less per seat while still making just as much (or more!) money.

Back to reality...

Reality goggles can be less fun.

However, there's a flaw in this logic. By the estimates of Optimism employee Kelvin Fichter, the vast majority of the L2 blockspace consumption comes from publishing transaction calldata. What if the so-called rollups decide not to publish this calldata to Ethereum? After all, L2s can publish transaction calldata to any storage device that their users trust.

You retort, lips trembling, "they wouldn't possibly think of doing that! It would ruin their Eth-alginment!" Well, I think they would. But more importantly, they already have. Matter Labs, the company behind zkSync, has proposed their own homegrown data availability layer in order to cheapen costs for users. Here's what they say about it:

Both parts will be composable and interoperable: contracts and accounts on the zkRollup side will be able to seamlessly interact with accounts on the zkPorter side, and vice versa. That’s right! From a user’s perspective, the only perceptible difference will be a 100x reduction in fees for zkPorter accounts.

Hence, Ethereum blockspace would no longer be enchanted with the magical spell of network effects. Instead, they would have to compete for user dollars with optimized data availability layers like Celestia. This is a fight that I think they will lose.

Conclusion

To be clear, I'm not hoping that Ethereum fails. I trace my own lineage back to the Ethereum ecosystem, and there are parts of the culture that I love. If the core engineers can execute on on EIP-4488 and make Eth the best data availability layer the world has ever seen, Ethereum will continue to have a big role in the rollup-centric world.

But I also think that we all benefit when the best technology wins. And if that best technology happens to be something other than Ethereum, so be it.
