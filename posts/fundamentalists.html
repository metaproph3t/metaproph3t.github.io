The fundamentalists and the narrativists

During my time in crypto, I've come to make a distinction between two types of people. I call the first group fundamentalists and the second narrativists.

Based on the names, you might be able to guess what the terms mean. Fundamentalists tend to focus on the fundamentals of things. Narrativists, on the other hand, are more focused on the thoughts of their peers. A fundamentalist investor, for instance, will tend to be have investment criteria like whether or not a business will provide something that is useful, whether or not the business is defensible, and the size of the market. On the other hand, a narrativist investor will be more focused on things like whether other investors have already invested, whether they can sell the investment to their investment committee (IC), and if they think other investors will follow them.

Many of the founders of foundational projects are fundamentalists. I would consider Satoshi, Vitalik, and Anatoly fundamentalists. Each gained notoriety for solving a real problem that they wanted fixed. For Satoshi, this was building a decentralized electronic cash system; for Vitalik, this was creating a turing-complete blockchain that could trustlessly perform arbitrary state transitions, and for Anatoly this was optimizing the validator internals such that a turing-complete blockchain could perform state transitions at scale.

A smaller but still sizeable portion of investors are fundamentalists. Kyle Samani and Olaf Carlson-Wee are two that come to mind. But most are narrativists. This is especially true of the VCs that came to crypto in peak hype times, like 2021 and 2017-2018. This is one of the reasons why the prices go so high in the good times and so low in the bad times: most people are buying or selling not based on fundamental conviction but based on the thoughts of their peers.

And almost all of the popular CT accounts are narrativists. For example, @cobie, @HsakaTrades, @DegenSpartan, @TrustlessState, and Adam Cochran are narrativists.

Usually, the narrativists want to be perceived as fundamentalists, and so adopt messaging strategies to provide cover for narrativist actions. For example, whenever I talk to VCs who were optimistic in 2021 but are now pessimistic on crypto, they almost never say "other people started being pessimistic, so I thought it would be a good idea to be pessimistic too." They say something like "the market shrunk" or point to the proverbial "regulatory overhang." Most of these pass as almost-truths, which is the point. Of course, they don't fool fundamentalists, who in these examples would understand that the market that crypto is trying to capture (financial services) is still ripe for disruption and that regulation has always been, and will always be, a concern in crypto since crypto is fundamentally a subversive force.

Although the fundamentalists don't generally want to be perceived as narrativists, some of them do wish to harness the powers of the narrativists.
