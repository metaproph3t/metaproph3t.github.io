Getting to Gaia

Introduction

In Isaac Asimov's sci-fi Foundation series, there is a planet called Gaia. From the outside, Gaia looks similar to Earth. Just like Earth, it has oceans, land, plants, and all the animals of the animal kingdom, including humans. But Gaia is different in one very important way. On Gaia, all matter is part of a broader group consciousness.

If an economist were visiting this super-organism, they might make the observation that 'on Gaia, all externalities are internalized.' That is, Gaians feel the impact of their actions on others. This is unlike Earth, where for example Alice may hurt Bob without feeling Bob's pain.

And the behavior described by Asimov is exactly what an economist would predict: every Gaian takes actions based on whether they will benefit Gaia as a whole. { insert passage }

Now, this all may sound very utopian and non-Earthy. After all, us Earthies have many situations where we can do something that's good for us but bad for others, or vice versa. These are the prisoner's dilemma incentive traps taking forms as varied as overfishing, NIMBYism, bad policies making for good politics, and underallocation of resources into public goods. The cynic may conclude "nothing changes, we will forever be doomed to our fate."

The flaw in the cynic's reasoning is that some places on Earth are more Gaian than others. Germany, for example, is more Gaian than Somalia. While the Germans live in a state of relative peace and prosperity, the Somalis live in anarchy and poverty. If we are forever doomed to our fate, why have some places figured out how to partially mitigate that fate?

See, although the humans on Earth are psychologically a far cry from the humans on Gaia, we have figured out a clever hack to emulate the Gaian success: institutions. The difference between Germany and Somalia is that Germany has institutions (e.g., property rights, the force of law) that encourage its citizens to contribute to overall welfare. Someone who might grow up to be a raider or warlord in Somalia might become a factory-worker or business owner in Germany.

But, as those who live in the world's wealthiest countries know, we still have a long way to go before we reach the Gaian ideal. This, in my view, is the central thrust behind the crypto movement. That is, crypto exists to provide alternative institutions that are more Gaian than present ones.

Crypto exists to provide alternative institutions...

If we break this claim into two halves, we can start by proving the first: that crypto exists to provide alternative institutions.

Crypto produces institutions

Crypto, unlike many industries, does not produce anything tangible. You can't eat blockspace, or use fraud proofs to travel from London to Shanghai, or clothe yourself in bitcoins.

Instead, crypto provides institutions. Some examples include:

  • Bitcoin, an institution for money defined by a supply cap of 21 million and peer-to-peer management of the ledger.
  • Ethereum and Solana, institutions that aim to provide 'world computers,' highly fault-tolerant and censorship- resistant state machines.
  • Nouns DAO, an institution built around the idea of a community with a treasury funded by new entrants.
  • MakerDAO, an institution controlled partly by code and partly by the wishes of MKR token-holders that aims to provide a decentralized stablecoin.

Some may remark: "these aren't institutions, they're software!" But the existence of blockchain forks illustrates that they are, in fact, institutions. When the DAO fork happened, both Ethereum and Ethereum Classic were practically identical software. So what made one Ethereum, and one Ethereum Classic? Simply, that we had decided it so. And consequently, while 1 DAI on Ethereum was still worth 1 US Dollar, 1 DAI on Ethereum classic was not. Code may be a part of our new cybernetic institutions, but it is not the only part.

The institutions are alternative ones

Importantly, these are alternative institutions. In other words, they hope to co-exist with or replace mainstream institutions, not reform them. Bitcoin is not led by central banks nor are DeFi protocols led by TradFi firms. Crypto is exit, not voice.

...that are more Gaian than present ones

This is where some of you might start to disagree with me. In my view, crypto doesn't exist in service of abstract ideals like 'freedom' or 'permissionlessness.' The goal is to build institutions that advance the collective welfare of society.

The Bitcoin case study

Bitcoin was the match that lit this fire, so it may be worth considering why it was created. Here's Satoshi, in his first post on the Bitcoin forum: "The central bank must be trusted not to debase the currency, but the history of fiat currencies is full of breaches of that trust. Banks must be trusted to hold our money and transfer it electronically, but they lend it out in waves of credit bubbles with barely a fraction in reserve. We have to trust them with our privacy, trust them not to let identity thieves drain our accounts" (italics mine).

Thus, although the Gospel According to Satoshi was imbued with hints of abstract ideals (e.g., privacy), clearly Bitcoin was made to be practical. It was made to solve the real problems of currency debasement and credit bubbles. Note that in the first Bitcoin block, Satoshi invoked 'Chancellor on the Brink of Second Bailout for Banks' and not a passage from The Road to Serfdom.

This is what makes Bitcoin worth considering. If it were simply 'anti-AML coin,' it might be interesting to a few drug lords and Ayn Rand book clubs. But if an economy run on bitcoins is less susceptible to currency debasement and credit bubbles, Bitcoin may bring humanity closer to Gaia.

Bitcoin also exemplifies the tools of the typical crypto-institution: power structures and incentives. By having the network run by a P2P set of nodes, Bitcoin ensures that 'elites' won't be print money for private benefit and socialized loss. By using block rewards, Bitcoin incentivizes people to contribute to the security and economic finality of the ledger.

Turing-complete blockchains as the Gaian nursery

Still, the skeptic may ask, "what role do blockchains have to play in this?" Fundamentally, turing-complete blockchains allow institution-designers to experiment without asking for permission.

To demonstrate, let's take the example of what I'm working on, futarchy. The basic idea of futarchy is to allow markets, instead of individuals, to make decisions. So if a corporation was deciding whether to fire the CEO, they could run two markets for their stock - 'CEO pass stock' and 'CEO fail stock' - and just do whichever ends up with the higher price. Now imagine what you would need to do in order to try futarchy in the world of legacy institutions. You would first need to hire some expensive lawyers to craft a special-purpose operating agreement, then you would need to find a government that would allow you to create an entity like this, then you would need to convince the local stock market to list these special markets, and on and on and on. Meanwhile, I, as a solo developer, will have created a futarchic organization on a public blockchain (Solana) in a few months of development time. The difference is night and day.

The point is not that futarchy will work. Like any experimental project, it carries a substantial risk of failure. The point is that such experimental projects are not possible in the existing legal / financial system. Instead of a small army of lawyers and lobbyists, you only need one programmer who wants to see an institution tried.

Conclusion

All these experiments are likely to lead to many failures. Crypto has already had plenty of those, both at a small scale and at a large scale. We will likely have many more. But in the end, the difference between the present-day and a crypto-enabled world could be as big as the difference between Somalia and Germany. I think it'll have been worth it.
