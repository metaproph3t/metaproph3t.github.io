Getting to Gaia

Introduction

In Isaac Asimov's Foundation series, there is a planet called Gaia. On Gaia, everyone is part of a singular group consciousness. The birds, the bugs, the trees, and the humans are mere organs of a larger organism.

Gaia has an interesting economic property: all externalities are internalized. Unlike Earth, where it is perfectly possible for a human to ignore their impact on other people, Gaiains feel their impact on other people. If a Gaian does something that benefits other Gaians, they reap that full benefit. Inversely, if a Gaian does something that hurts other Gaians, they feel the full brunt of the pain they have caused.

And the behavior described by Asimov is exactly what an economist would predict: every Gaian takes actions based on whether they will benefit Gaia as a whole. { insert passage }

Now, this all may sound very utopian and non-Earthy. After all, we have plenty of prisoner's dilemma incentive traps that we fall into. We have overfishing, NIMBYism, bad policies making for good politics, and underallocation of resources into public goods. You may conclude from this that us Earthies are doomed to our fate of constant fighting, theft, and politicking. After all, our psychology is unlikely to change, given that it has formed via millions of years of evolution.

The flaw in this reasoning is that we do have some places that are more Gaian than others. Although Germany, for example, might not be perfectly Gaian, I think we can all agree that it is more Gaian than, say, Somalia. Is this because Germans are intrinsically superior to Somalians? Well, no. See, even though humans are psychologically a far cry from the Gaiains, we have figured out a clever hack to emulate the Gaian success: institutions. Germany is more prosperous because its institutions encourage people to contribute to general wellfare more often than in Somalia. You can't become wealthy by becoming a German warlord, for example.

This brings us to the topic of today's discussion: why we need crypto. I think this is an important one, given that many people are currently jaded on crypto's ability to provide anything that benefits humanity. My view is that crypto exists to provide alternative institutions that are more Gaian than present ones. I will devote the remainder of the essay to proving this claim.

Crypto exists to provide alternative institutions...

If we break this claim into two halves, we can start by proving the first: that crypto exists to provide alternative institutions.

Crypto, unlike many industries, does not produce anything tangible. You can't eat blockspace, or use fraud proofs to travel from London to Shanghai, or clothe yourself in bitcoins.

Instead, crypto provides institutions. Some examples include:

  • Bitcoin, an institution for money defined by a supply cap of 21 million and peer-to-peer management of the ledger.
  • Ethereum and Solana, institutions that aim to provide 'world computers,' highly fault-tolerant and censorship- resistant state machines.
  • Nouns DAO, an institution built around the idea of a community with a treasury funded by new entrants.
  • MakerDAO, an institution controlled partly by code and partly by the wishes of MKR token-holders that aims to provide a decentralized stablecoin.

Now, some may remark: "these aren't institutions, they're software!" But consider the case of the MakerDAO-deployed smart contracts on the Ethereum Proof-of-Work (PoW) chain. These contracts have the same code as that of Ethereum mainnet. And yet 1 DAI on EthereumPoW is not redeemable for 1 US Dollar. As the saying goes, "it all boils down to social consensus." Code may be a part of our cybernetic institutions, but it is not the only part.

Importantly, these are alternative institutions. They aim to co-exist with or replace mainstream institutions, not reform them. Although some projects have tried to 'put the existing world on blockchain rails,' like Hyperledger Fabric, most have failed or are failing.

...that are more Gaian than present ones

The goal is not simply to build alternative institutions for the sake of it, but to build better ones. Ones that allow us to get closer to the Gaian ideal.

Here's Satoshi in his first post on Bitcointalk: "The central bank must be trusted not to debase the currency, but the history of fiat currencies is full of breaches of that trust. Banks must be trusted to hold our money and transfer it electronically, but they lend it out in waves of credit bubbles with barely a fraction in reserve. We have to trust them with our privacy, trust them not to let identity thieves drain our accounts" (italics mine).

Evidently, Bitcoin was made to be practical. It wasn't about "the chains of the state" as much as it was about "here's a real problem with money, here's a solution." Importantly, the first Bitcoin block contained 'Chancellor on the brink of second round of bailouts' and not a passage from The Road to Serfdom. If Bitcoin was merely a less-permissioned money, with no practical benefits to society, it might have adopters among the world's drug markets and Ayn Rand book clubs, but it wouldn't be particularly interesting. What makes Bitcoin interesting is that legacy money institutions have a long history of hurting people, and that Bitcoin could take away their power.

What does this all have to do with Gaia? Well, on Gaia, every person takes actions based on how they will impact the world as a whole. Since humans on Earth have been trained by millions of years of natural selection to not think this way, all we can do is try to emulate the Gaian ideal.

For example, we can incentivize people to do socially-beneficial things, so that greed leads to good. We can also change the way that power is allocated. Most crypto-institutions use a combination of these two in order to achive their goals. For example, Bitcoin allocates power to its users to solve the aforemention problems with centralized control over money supply and incentivizes network security (concretely, the money working) via block rewards.

While changing power and incentive structures sounds somewhat removed from reality, power and incentive structures are the main thing separating the prosperous Western world and the less-prosperous countries, such as those in Africa. In order to become very rich in Swaziland, one must befriend the king; in order to become very rich in America, one must solve a big problem for millions of people. This, and not other less important factors like culture, is why Swaziland founders while America prospers. The difference between the present-day world and one that is closer to Gaia may be just as big as the difference between Swaziland and the West.
