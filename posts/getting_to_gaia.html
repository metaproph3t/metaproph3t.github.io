Getting to Gaia

Introduction

In Isaac Asimov's Foundation series, there is a planet called Gaia. On Gaia, everyone is part of a singular group consciousness. It is therefore meaningless to speak of 'Gaians' - there is only Gaia, with its many organs taking the form of birds, bugs, trees, and humans.

In an economist's eyes, this planet has an interesting property: all externalities are internalized. That is, when a human hurts another human, they feel their pain as it is their own. On the flipside, when someone makes another person happy, they get to share in that happiness. The result of this is that every action is taken based on whether it benefits Gaia as a whole. Because of this, Prisoner's dilemma incentive traps, rampant on earth in forms as varied as overfishing, NIMBYism, bad policies making for good politics, and underallocation of resources into public goods, don't exist on Gaia.

A few days ago on our home planet of Earth, Erik Voorhees gave a talk entitled 'Why are we here?' in which he laid out his views on what exactly the crypto movement is providing for humanity. His principal claim is that we are here to bring about more freedom and permissionless-ness for humanity.

As well-spoken as his speech was, I have a different view on why we are here. My view is that crypto exists to provide alternative institutions that are more Gaia-like than present ones.

Crypto exists to provide alternative institutions...

If we break this claim into two halves, we can start by proving the first: that crypto exists to provide alternative institutions.

Crypto, unlike many industries, does not produce anything tangible. You can't eat blockspace, or use fraud proofs to travel from London to Shanghai, or clothe yourself in bitcoins. Neither does crypto provide information or allow you to efficiently store it - you can't use it to find barbers in your area and it is not recommended that you use it as a drop-in replacement for MySQL.

Instead, crypto provides institutions. Bitcoin is an institution for money defined by a supply cap of 21 million and peer-to-peer management of the ledger. Ethereum and Solana are institutions that aim to provide highly fault-tolerant and censorship-resistant state machines - 'world computers.' Nouns DAO is an institution built around the idea of cultural influence.

Importantly, these are alternative institutions. That is, they aim to co-exist with or replace mainstream institutions, like banks, corporations, and nation-states. They generally do not attempt to reform those institutions. In the mental model of 'voice vs. exit,' crypto is exit. Although there have been some attempts to get legacy institutions involved in crypto and blockchains, most have either not worked or are currently flailing. Think Hyperledger Fabric, Hedera Hashgraph, and Ripple.

...that are more Gaia-like than present ones

The goal is not simply to build alternative institutions for the sake of it, but to build better ones. Ones that allow us to get closer to the Gaian ideal.

Here's Satoshi in his first post on Bitcointalk: "The central bank must be trusted not to debase the currency, but the history of fiat currencies is full of breaches of that trust. Banks must be trusted to hold our money and transfer it electronically, but they lend it out in waves of credit bubbles with barely a fraction in reserve. We have to trust them with our privacy, trust them not to let identity thieves drain our accounts" (italics mine). Evidently, Bitcoin was made to be practical. It wasn't about "the chains of the state" as much as it was about "here's a real problem with money, here's a solution." Importantly, the first Bitcoin block contained 'Chancellor on the brink of second round of bailouts' and not a passage from The Road to Serfdom. If Bitcoin was merely a less-permissioned money, with no practical benefits to society, it might have adopters among the world's drug markets and Ayn Rand book clubs, but it wouldn't be particularly interesting. What makes Bitcoin interesting is that legacy money institutions have a long history of hurting people, and that Bitcoin could take away their power.

What does this all have to do with Gaia? Well, on Gaia, every person takes actions based on how they will impact the world as a whole. Since humans on Earth have been trained by millions of years of natural selection to not think this way, all we can do is try to emulate the Gaian ideal. For example, we can incentivize people to do socially-beneficial things, so that greed leads to good. Indeed, much of what separates the prosperous Western world and the less-prosperous continents and sub-continents is the incentives involved. In order to become very rich in Swaziland, one must befriend the king; in order to become very rich in America, one must solve a big problem for millions of people. We can also change the way that power is allocated. Most crypto-institutions have a combination of these two; for example, Bitcoin uses block rewards and PoW so that greedy miners increase the network's security and pushes power to its users to solve the aforementioned problems with centralized control over the money supply.

We have strayed from the path and need to re-find our way

While I have great respect for Voorhees, I think his speech was emblematic of the shift we need in crypto's spiritual leadership. It has been 14 years since Bitcoin was invented. 14 years! And what do we have to show for it? Certainly not nothing - we have stablecoins that you can send instantly around the world, we have some financial primitives that could lay the bedrock of a future financial system, and we have some green shoots when it comes to RWAs and RWA adoption. But I feel that we are capable of so much more.

One of the reasons why I am happy to be in the Solana community is that it seems to be a bright spot vis-a-vis the need to be useful for the world.
